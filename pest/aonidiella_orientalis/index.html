Aonidiella orientalis

Aonidiella orientalis (Newstead)

Taxonomic placing: Insecta, Hemimetabola, Hemiptera, Coccoidea, Diaspididae.

Common name: Oriental red scale.

Geographical distribution: South-East Asia, Australia, parts of Africa, Central America, Middle East. In Israel since 1980, now found in most mango-growing regions. Commonwealth Institute of Entomology Map #386, 1978.

Economic importance: A pest of many crops, including avocado, citrus, coconut, papaya and tea. In Israel A. orientalis is a minor pest of mango, settling on the leaves and twigs. The feeding sites on the fruit remain green even after color break, reducing their market value.

Host plants: Very polyphagous.

Morphology: The dorsal macroducts are one-barred, slender, usually three on each side of the pygidium. The pre-pygidial abdominal segments carry clusters of microducts. The female is rounded, initially yellow, later turning brown-red. After mating the fused prosoma and thorax become sclerotized, except for a central area extending from the pygidium to the mouth parts. The pygidium bears three lobes, the two median lobes externally and internally notched, the third only externally; the fourth lobe occurs as a mere point. The anus is located about two-thirds of the distance between the vulva and the median lobes. Three small paraphyses arise near the second and third lobes on either side of the pygidium. The ventral vulva lies between two clusters of about 10 perivulvar pores on either side, with 1-2 additional pores in front.

The shield is thick, flat, gray-white to brownish, round, the darker juvenile exuviae almost at center. The winged males are pale-orange, their dark shields oval, the paler exuvium of the 1st instar placed near one end.

Life cycle: The pest settles on mango leaves, twigs and fruit. Its populations peak in the winter, are low in the spring and increasing during late summer. The scale is more abundant on tree sides not exposed to the prevailing winds.

Management: The oriental red scale may be controlled by a mixture of two pesticides, an organophosphate and a carbamate.

Biological control: The oriental red scale is attacked by several hymenopterous endoparasitoids, including Comperiella bifasciata Howard and Habrolepis aspidioti Compere and Annecke, the aphelinid ectoparasitoids Aphytis melinus DeBach and Aphytis lingnanensis Compere, and the endoparasitoid Encarsia sp. It is not clear whether they contribute to the control of the pest.
