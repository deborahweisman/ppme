Typhlodromus athiasae

Typhlodromus athiasae Porath & Swirski

Taxonomic placing: Acari , Mesostigmata, Phytoseiidae .

Geographical distribution: Middle East, Greece and Libya.

Host plants: Many indigenous and introduced, annual and perennial plants.

Morphology: Length of the reticulated dorsum about 0.3 mm, with 17 pairs of nude setae plus two pairs of lateral setae. All dorsal setae on the anterior part of the body subequal in length, the posterior setae longer. A single macroseta present on leg IV. The sternal plate with four pairs of setae, and there are four pairs of preanal setae.

Economic importance: In Israel Typhlodromus athiasae is the dominant phytoseiid predator of the European red mite, Panonychus ulmi Koch on apples , and often reduces its populations. It is also the dominant phytoseiid on citrus trees in the inner parts of Israel and in the Nile Delta region of Egypt.

Life history: Typhlodromus athiasae raised a generation in 11-12 days at 25Â°C and produced about 30 eggs in 20 days at that temperature and 70% relative humidity. Fecundity was reduced at higher or lower relative humidities. The pollen of Carpobrotus edulis (Aizoaceae) and the spider mite Tetranychus urticae Koch provided the best nutrients for reproduction, but the mite can also survive and lay eggs on many other foods. A single mating sufficed for fertilizing all female eggs. The larvae did not feed but other stages were cannibalistic. The mite does not have a winter diapause.
