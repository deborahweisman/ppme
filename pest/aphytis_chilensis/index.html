Aphytis chilensis

Aphytis chilensis Howard

Taxonomic placing: Insecta, Holometabola, Hymenoptera, Chalcidoidea, Aphelinidae.

Geographical distribution: Cosmopolitan.

Hosts: Several armored scale insects (Diaspididae).

Economic importance: An important parasitoid of the oleander scale, Aspidiotus nerii Bouche.

Morphology: The female is yellow, its size being from 0.5 to 1.1 mm; smaller specimens are paler, bear less setae and were even described as a separate species (Aphytis riadi).

Life cycle: Like other members of its genus, Aphytis chilensis is an ectoparasitoid of armored scale insects. The 2nd-stage nymphs, young females and scale prepupae are attacked, but the ovipositing females are the preferred stage for parasitization. The eggs are deposited through the shield onto the host’s body.

(Figure 1)

Due to the fact that at molting the shields of these insects adhere to their bodies, the parasitoid can deposit only between molts. Its emerging larvae feed on the scales and prior to pupation they excrete their fecal matter (meconia). The pupa is free, and its eyes change from colorless to red to green, providing an indication of its age. The adult often emerges through an exit hole in the shield, or by crawling out from underneath. Although reared (in the field and in the laboratory) from several hosts, it seems to prefer the oleander scale. Aphytis chilensis is thelytokous (but males have been described), requiring ca 18 days to complete a generation at 28°C and each female produces about 25 offspring. In the field the parasitoid has two population peaks, in spring and in autumn. It attacks mainly young host females and is a voracious host feeder, killing more scales by this practice than by parasitization.

Water extract of the shield of the oleander scale elicited a positive response from A. chilensis, suggesting that its host-searching is based on chemical cues.
