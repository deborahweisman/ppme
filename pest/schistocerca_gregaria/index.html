Schistocerca gregaria

Schistocerca gregaria (Forskal)

Taxonomic placing: Insecta, Hemimetabola, Orthoptera, Acridoidea, Acrididae, Cyrtacanthacridinae.

Common name: Desert locust.

Geographical distribution: ranging from West Africa, across the Arabian peninsula and most of the Indian sub-continent. In the Mediterranean region: all of northern Africa, the Middle East north to parts of Turkey, Iran and the Central Asian Republics.

Host plants: highly polyphagous, feeding mainly on a large variety of rangeland shrubs and grasses that grow in primary breeding sites and on economic crops when encountered during pest outbreaks.

Economic importance: Damage caused by the desert locust is a consequence of its polyphagous nature, the density of the population, and its habits to aggregate and swarm. Each individual gregarious locust is able to consume roughly its own weight in foliage daily. This partly explains how dense swarms of adults, or marching bands of nymphs, can inflict considerable injury in a short time. Damage by gregarious swarming populations occurs in cycles, in most cases afflicting economies in fragile ecosystems, least able to sustain biomass reduction or complete crop loss. In the long term, the cumulative damage does not surpass that inflicted by indigenous grasshoppers.

Morphology: When reared in isolation and consequently solitariform, locusts exhibit convex pronotal crests, typical morphometric ratios of body measurements, and cryptic green to light-brown coloration usually harmonizing with the color of the immediate environment and affected by humidity. Cuticular accumulation of melanin is markedly reduced. In contrast gregariform locusts, crowded during rearing, have concave or straight pronota, and are yellow with contrasted melanized and reddish marking. When adult but immature, the background color of both sexes of the desert locust has a pink caste. The mature adult gregariform male is yellowish, progressing to bright yellow, that of females brownish. Solitariform adults retain their green to brown coloration and convex pronota.

Life cycle: Eggs of the desert locust develop and hatch in about two weeks. Gregariform nymphs pass through five instars before maturing to the adult stage, while solitariform nymphs pass through six instars. Solitariform individuals tend to disperse and exhibit low activity. Gregariform nymphs tend to aggregate, and they are more active than solitariform nymphs.

Ecology: Nymphal development requires about one month under optimal conditions, but may extend for 2-3 months under less favorable conditions. Newly-eclosed adults are reproductively immature. During their pre-reproductive period the adults accumulate reserves in preparation for migration and/or reproduction. Reproductive maturation occurs within about three weeks but may be delayed for many months, in synchrony with the environment. There is evidence that delayed maturation within primary breeding sites is synchronized with rainfall and flowering of desert shrubs.

During periods of recession, desert locusts breed in primary breeding sites, mainly restricted to semi-arid and arid deserts where precipitation is sporadic and below 200 mm annually. Rainfall in primary breeding sites initiates seed germination. This sequence of events subsequently leads to an increased population density. Within 2-3 generations of increasing density, transformation to the gregariform phase is induced. When these conditions cease, and a dry spell occurs, adults swarm gregariously, migrating actively in the direction of prevailing winds. In Africa, migration is associated with the Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ). When opposing winds meet within this zone, high air pressure develops and vertical flow of air occurs. During its ascent, air temperature decreases leading to an increase in relative humidity and finally to rainfall. The arrival of locust swarms to the convergence zone coincides with the occurrence of precipitation and vegetation, encouraging and supporting further population growth.

Females contribute to gregarization by depositing egg pods close to pods that are being laid concurrently or have been laid previously. This is due to their attraction to ovipositional sites, whether because of suitability of soil, moisture and other environmental conditions, or to attraction to an oviposition pheromone with which each female marks the exact spot wherein she has deposited eggs. Clutches of eggs are bound together with a proteinaceous foam, which is produced in the female genital accessory glands. Solitarious females produce and deposit more eggs (60-80) in each clutch than do gregarious females (40-60).

Swarming behavior and migration are not necessarily dependent on gregariform phase characteristics of coloration or morphometrics, but are usually positively correlated to population density: Gregarious behavior is induced as a consequence of interactions among individuals upon crowding, irrespective of whether they are initially solitariform or gregariform in coloration and morphometrics. Solitarious behavior is conversely expressed when locusts are isolated, even when initially gregariform. Solitarious larvae do not aggregate, whereas gregarious locusts are marked with bright color patterns, tend to aggregate in their nymphal stages and to march together in bands, while adults fly in dense and highly mobile swarms. Locust flight is directed by prevailing winds. These directions differ from night to day. Locusts ascend on thermal currents during the day to considerable heights, where they may encounter wind directions differing from those found at lower altitudes.

The area covered by gregarious locust swarms may range from 1 to 200 square kilometers, at a density which is commonly in the range of 100 individual per square meter, but under extreme conditions may be one to two orders of magnitude greater in density.

Another characteristic of sustained flight or migration, is that reproduction is normally delayed. This may be due to the allocation for flight of limited resources in the first case, or allocation of the same resource pool for egg development, in the second. This differential allocation of resources is termed the oocyte-flight syndrome.

Management

Control: Populations of the Desert Locust are monitored during recession and outbreak by various international agencies. These include the Desert Locust Control Organization (DLCO) of the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) of the United Nations. When gregarization commences, outbreak is prevented primarily by chemical control, preferably with less persistent chemicals, such as the pyrethroids fenitrothion and deltamethrin, the insect growth regulator (IGR) diflubenzuron, the carbamate bendiocarb and the GABA-blocker fipronil. Neem seed extracts, whether prepared locally or commercially, are effective as feeding deterrents of the desert locust.

The best chance to restrict locust outbreak is by careful monitoring and control of evolving gregarization. There are several reasons why this is not always logistically possible, or indeed efficient. Primarily, this is because breeding sites are sporadic and spread over a very wide area, often in remote location. Access to many of these sites is difficult, security questionable, and resources limited. Finally, the cycles of desert locust outbreaks and recessions are extended, and this leads to loss of attention during extended recession periods. There is a recurrent debate concerning the long-term economic justification of global locust control.

Biological Control: Various parasites and predators have been reported to attack the different stages of Desert Locust development. None are effective in suppressing populations once they have gregarized and swarmed. They may contribute to some degree to the collapse of locust populations, once the outbreak had peaked. Recent studies with preparations of the entomopathogenic fungus Metarhizium anisopliae are encouraging.
