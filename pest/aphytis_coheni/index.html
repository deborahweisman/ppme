Aphytis coheni

Aphytis coheni DeBach

Taxonomic placing: Insecta, Hymenoptera, Chalcidoidea, Aphelinidae.

Common name: None.

Geographical distribution: Probably a native of south-east Asia that had fortuitously arrived in Israel and Lebanon.

Hosts: California red scale, Aonidiella aurantii (Maskell), rarely other armored scale insects (Diaspididae).

Economic importance: An important natural enemy of the California red scale, especially in the inner, warmer and drier parts of Israel.

Morphology: The female is yellow, about 0.8 to 1.1 mm in length.

Life cycle: Like other members of its genus, Aphytis coheni is an ectoparasitoid of armored scale insects. The 2nd-stage nymphs, young females and scale prepupae are attacked, but the young ovipositing females are the preferred stage for parasitization. The eggs are deposited through the shield on the host’s body; due to the fact that when molting the shields of these scales adhere to their bodies, the parasitoid can deposit only between molts. Its emerging larvae feed on the scales and prior to pupation they excrete their fecal matter in the shape of black pellets (meconia). The pupa is free, its eyes changing from colorless to red to green, providing an indication of its age. The adult often emerges through an exit hole in the shield, or by crawling out from underneath. The California red scale is its major host, although the ectoparasitoid was also obtained from a few other armored scale hosts. Aphytis coheni is arrhenotokous, requiring 3-4 weeks at 20°C to complete a generation, 12 days at 28°C. Each female, whether mated or not, produces about 25 offspring; the parasitoid may place an average of 5 eggs in such hosts. At 24°C males live about three weeks, females slightly longer. Aphytis coheni is a voracious host feeder that kills twice as many scales by this practice than by parasitization.

In the field the populations of A. coheni have a mild spring peak. Its juveniles, which are protected during their development by the host’s shield, seem to be tolerant to high temperatures, but the adults, and especially the males, are very susceptible. The parasitoid’s presence in the inner, hotter and drier regions of Israel is probably due to lack of competition there from other parasitoids of the California red scale (which are more susceptible to arid conditions), not due to any intrinsic superior biological attributes.

Water extracts of the shield of the California red scale elicited positive responses from A. coheni, indicating that its host searching is based on chemical cues.
