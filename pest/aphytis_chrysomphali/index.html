Aphytis chrysomphali

Aphytis chrysomphali (Mercet)

Taxonomic placing: Insecta, Holometabola, Hymenoptera, Chalcidoidea, Aphelinidae.

Common name: None.

Geographical distribution: Probably a native of the Mediterranean basin; its distribution is now almost cosmopolitan.

Hosts: Several armored scale insects (Diaspididae), especially California red scale, Aonidiella aurantii (Maskell).

Economic importance: A member of a guild of parasitoids that attack the California red scale. In Israel and Cyprus A. chrysomphali occurs only along the coastal plain.

Morphology: The female is yellow, its size is from 0.6 to 1.0 mm.

Life cycle: Like other members of its genus, A. chrysomphali is an ectoparasitoid of armored scale insects. The 2nd-stage nymphs, young females and scale prepupae are attacked, but the ovipositing females are the preferred stage for parasitization. The eggs are deposited through the shield on the host’s body; due to the fact that when molting the shields of these scales adhere to their bodies, the ectoparasitoid can deposit only between molts. Its emerging larvae feed on the scales and prior to pupation they excrete their fecal matter in the shape of black pellets (”meconia”). The pupa is free, its eyes changing from colorless to red to green, providing an indication of age. The adult often emerges through an exit hole in the host’s shield, or by crawling out from underneath. Although obtained from several armored scale hosts, it is best known as a parasitoid of the California red scale. Aphytis chrysomphali is a uniparental thelytokous species (with rare males) and a voracious host feeder, killing more scales by this practice than by parasitization. It completes a cycle in a fortnight at 25Â°C, at which temperature each female produces about 25 offspring. It appears to be tolerant to low temperatures but susceptible to heat and aridity, attributes that could explain its prevalence along the coastal plain.
