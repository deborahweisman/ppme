Phyllocoptruta oleivora

Phyllocoptruta oleivora (Ashmead)

Taxonomic placing: Acari, Prostigmata, Eriophyoidea Eriophyidae

Common name: Citrus rust mite, silver mite.

Geographical distribution: Almost wherever citrus is grown. Commonwealth Institute of Entomology Map #78, 1978 (revised).

Host plants: Citrus spp.; Swinglea glutinosa ; Fortunella spp.

Morphology: The citrus rust mite is wedge-shaped, yellowish in color, about 0.15 mm in length, with a pair of very short prodorsal setae. The prodorsum bears a six-sided cell and the opisthosoma is marked by a distinct dorsal furrow. The featherclaw is five-rayed.

Symptoms and economic importance: The vagrant mites feed on the fruit, leaves and green twigs, piercing the epidermal cells and killing them. Rust-like blemishes that consist of masses of dead cells, appear on the affected areas on orange, grapefruit and mandarin fruits; on lemons and citron they are silvery. As infestation proceeds the surface of the fruit becomes russetted and bronzed, rough and scaly; peels of young fruit lose their elasticity and may shrivel. Dark spots appear on affected leaves, which may also lose their gloss, and their lower surfaces become rust-like. Infested green twigs may show blackish areas.
The mite cannot feed on the russetted fruit rind and will not be found there; it should be looked for at the margins of the affected area, and is also very common around the sepals.

The citrus rust mite is a major pest of citrus; unless controlled it can affect 70-100% of the yield. Heavily infested fruit is smaller, its quality is reduced and it may drop; such fruit is rejected as fresh product and by the industry. Attack on leaves and twigs can affect next year’s growth.

Life cycle: The citrus rust mite develops throughout the year, its entire cycle (egg to egg) requiring about a week in summer, several weeks in winter, and the pest may raise 30 annual generations. The mite has four stages: egg, two nymphs and adults. Reproduction is indirect; spermatophores are deposited by males on the leaf substrate and taken up by the females. The species is arrhenotokous, each female depositing about a dozen eggs at a range of temperatures between 20-31°C.

Ecology: The citrus rust mite lives only on its host plants. Conditions optimal for its development are temperatures around 25°C and very high relative humidity. The mite is resistant to sub-freezing temperatures but susceptible to warm and dry winds. Consequently its populations (unless reduced by hot winds) increase during spring, remain high in the summer and decline in autumn. Mites (mostly females) disperse between patches when borne on winds and on infested plant material, such dispersal taking place throughout the year.

Management

Sampling: Before fruit are about 1.5 cm in diameter, the citrus rust mite will be found mostly on the underside of young leaves. Later the mites prefer the fruit, initially feeding at protected sites, and later on its entire surface. Orchards, or parts thereof where the pest occurred in former years, should be sampled early in the season. An examination of four fruits, taken from around the tree (with hand lens of 10X or more magnification), of 1 cm² per fruit, will provide a reasonable estimate of infestation, provided 10 or more mites are present in each sample. A rapid estimate of the population can also be obtained by using standardized visual keys based on estimating infestation rates per 1 cm² of plant surface and comparing them to the keys.

Crop resistance: Citrus varieties differ in their attractiveness to the citrus rust mite. Lemons, oranges, tangerines and rough lemon are very susceptible, whereas pummelo and sour orange seem to be resistant.

Chemical control: Due to the rapid rate of increase of the citrus rust mite, it is usually recommended to treat it as soon as the pest is recognized, especially at sites that had experienced damage in the past. A repeat spray may be needed within 4-6 weeks. Fruit intended for processing can tolerate some cosmetic damage and may require fewer chemical treatments than fruit sold on the fresh market. Acaricides in current common usage include bromopropylate (Neoron), dicofol, maneb, quinomethionate, abamectin and zineb compounds (e.g. metiram). Field resistance to zineb has been reported from Florida, Cyprus and Israel, and resistance to dicofol was found in Florida.

Natural enemies: Several predatory mites of the families Phytoseiidae and Stigmaeidae prey on the citrus rust mite, and may be able to control the pest in combination, especially if active during early spring. None, however, appears to be either specific or capable of predictably controlling the pest. The acaropathogenic fungus Hirsutella thompsonii periodically controls the citrus rust mite in Florida and has been used for this purpose.
