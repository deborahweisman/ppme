Therioaphis trifolii

Therioaphis trifolii (=maculata) (Monell)

Taxonomic placing: Insecta, Hemiptera, Sternorrhyncha, Aphidoidea, Aphididae.

Common name: Spotted alfalfa aphid.

Geographical distribution: The Mediterranean Basin, Central Asia and North America. Common Institute of Entomology Map #126, 1991 (revised).

Host plants: Alfalfa (Medicago) and clover (Trifolium).

Economic importance: Usually the spotted alfalfa aphid is a minor pest of alfalfa and clover in the Middle East, but occasionally it damages these crops. Heavily infested plants turn yellow (chlorotic) and an injected toxin, as well as a transmitted plant virus, aggravate the damage. In addition, plants become contaminated by the honeydew and subsequent sooty mold.

Morphology: The body of apterous and alate females is lemon-green and is covered by rows of tubercles (hence “spotted”). The darker siphunculi are very short and the cauda is truncated. Body length is 1.3-2.1 mm.

Life cycle: In the Middle East the aphid reproduces by viviparous parthenogenesis the year around, alate forms appearing when colonies become overcrowded. A generation may be completed in one week at 26.3ºC, the threshold of development is around 8ºC, and about 40 generation can annually be raised along the coastal plain of Israel. On the other hand, khamsin events greatly reduce the pest’s numbers.

The populations of the spotted alfalfa aphid consist of two biotypes, one apparently more suited to alfalfa (the spotted alfalfa aphid), the other more to clover (the spotted clover aphid).

Management

Plant resistance: Several commercial varieties of alfalfa are resistant to the pest.

Chemical control: The spotted alfalfa aphid has developed resistance to most organophosphates, but pyrethroids may still control it.

Biological control: The natural enemies of the aphid include predators such as Coccinellidae, Syrphidae and Chrysopidae, as well as several hymenopterous endoparasitoids of the family Aphidiidae, and entomopathogenetic fungi. Three Aphidiidae [an Aphelinus sp., Praon exsoletum (Nees) and Trioxys complanatus Quilis] were collected in Israel and successfully introduced into California to control the pest there. An indigenous strain of the fungus Zoophthora radicans (Brefeld) was obtained in Israel and successfully introduced into Australia to control the pest there.
