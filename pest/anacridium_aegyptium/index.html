Anacridium aegyptium

Anacridium aegyptium (L)

Taxonomy: Insecta, Hemimetabola , Orthoptera Acridoidea, Acrididae Cyrtacanthacridinae

Common name: Egyptian tree locust.

(Click on image to enlarge)

Geographical distribution: The Egyptian tree locust occurs in all countries that border the Mediterranean and that are included in this compendium, and eastwards into Pakistan.

Host plants: This polyphagous feeds on grapevines in enclosed facilities as well as on the foliage of citrus and deciduous orchard trees, and on vegetables, but rarely on graminaceous plants.

Economic importance: Anacridium aegyptium is generally regarded as a minor pest, but may occasionally be found in sufficient density to cause local damage.

Morphology: The Egyptian tree locust is a large grasshopper. At rest, the adult female body, including the folded tegmina, is up to 90 mm in length, while the male measures up to 65 mm. The mottled tegmina project beyond the end of the abdomen. Adults are grey-brown in color. The hind wings are transparent and have a purplish cast, and a curved band of darker aspect runs diagonally across them. A dull yellow line runs dorsally along the crest of the pronotum. The hind tibial spines are basally pale and apically dark-tipped. The juveniles are greenish.

Life cycle: The pest establishes one generation per year. The adult female undergoes a photoperiod-controlled reproductive diapause that lasts from September to January, and deposits eggs in the spring. Juveniles develop during the hot season, from April to August, with new adults appearing near the end of the summer.

Management: In most cases, control measures are not indicated for the occasional locust adults encountered. Because of their habit of roosting in trees, markedly during the winter diapause, it is not practical to control these pests with poisoned bait. If necessary, baits should be used against the juveniles during their development in the summer. Chemical control measures being developed against migratory locusts may be appropriate for control of these large grasshoppers.
