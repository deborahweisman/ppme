Phytoplasma

Phytoplasma

Over the course of the previous century pathogens causing “yellows” diseases were variously known as viruses, mycoplasma-like organisms (MLOs) and finally phytoplasmas. They have not yet been cultured in artificial media, which has greatly hindered research. However, they have been identified by several means, including antibodies, DNA probes and polymerase chain reaction (PCR) primers. A proposed classification scheme distinguishes phytoplasma clades at the level of genera, and subclades as species. Of the 14 phytoplasma groups, a number have been named in the genus Candidatus.

The symptoms of “yellows” diseases vary, depending on the host plant. Plant growth may be stunted or dwarfed, due to reduced shoot growth and lack of apical dominance. Leaves tend to be yellow, although they may also turn reddish, are smaller than normal, and often curl inwards. There may also be a proliferation of leaves known as witches’ broom, or production of “asparagus fern” branching. Symptoms often appear to be hormonally induced.

While phytoplasmas can be transmitted by cuttings, grafting, and parasitic plants such as dodder (Cuscuta pentagona), they are primarily spread by insects, mainly Hemiptera. Leafhoppers (Cicadelloidea) and planthoppers (Fulgoroidea) are the predominant transmitters of phytoplasmas, although Psyllidae and a member of the Flatidae are also known vectors.

Several traits combine to make these hemimetabolous insects efficient vectors of phytoplasmas. Their nymphs and adults feed on the same hosts and inhabit the same physical location, thus facilitate transmission by juveniles as well adults. They feed selectively on the tissues wherein phytoplasmas reside, namely phloem sieve elements. The insects’ feeding is non-destructive, promoting successful inoculation of the vascular system, without damaging the plant. Finally, the phytoplasmas replicate in the insect hosts and persist therein, and can therefore be transmitted for days, weeks or months after the initial acquisition.

The transmission cycle of phytoplasmas can be divided into two phases. 1. The acquisition time or the acquisition access period (AAP), the length of time an insect must feed on the plant in order to acquire the phytoplasma. Most insects require more than 24 hours for maximum acquisition and subsequent transmission of the pathogen. This period is age dependent (nymphs versus adults) and has to be established for each individual species. 2. The latent period (LP), the time required by the phytoplasma to penetrate the gut wall, migrate to the salivary glands, replicate and become transmissible. This period is temperature dependent. At optimal temperatures it can be as short as 10-20 days, whereas at low temperatures 50-60 days may be required. At high temperatures (relative to the particular phytoplasma, usually above 30°C), phytoplasmas are inactivated within the insect.

Relationships between vector and phytoplasmas are highly complex. Vectors can have dual infections; they may harbor a phytoplasma but be unable to vector it to healthy plants; or they may be unable to acquire a given phytoplasma from one plant species, but obtain it from another plant. Polyphagous vectors, such as Macrosteles fascifrons (Stål) (Cicadellidae), are capable to infecting a range of plants with a number of phytoplasmas, whereas monophagous or oligophagous vectors, such as Scaphoideus luteolus DeLong (Cicadellidae), can transmit phytoplasmas only to a few plant species, primarily Ulmus spp.
