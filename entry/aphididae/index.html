Aphididae

Aphididae

Taxonomic placing: Insecta, Hemimetabola, Hemiptera, Sternorrhyncha, Aphidoidea

Common name: Aphids, plant lice

Geographical distribution: World-wide, with close to 4,000 described species that are more abundant in the Holarctic region.

Morphology: Relatively small, soft-skinned insects with a clear separation into head, thorax, or abdomen, and functional sucking mouth parts in both sexes. The rostrum is made up of 4-5 segments and the antennae have 4-6 segments. The translucent wings, when present, bear reduced venation. The fifth or sixth abdominal segments carry a pair of dorsal siphunculi and the terminal segment bears an extension, the cauda. Adults may be alate (and then usually with darker bodies) or apterous.

Life Histories: All Aphididae are plant feeders and usually undergo four nymphal stages. They may lay eggs as well as being viviparous and show a diversity of developmental histories, species changing between parthenogenesis and sexual reproduction, and sometimes alternating between unrelated host plants. A major factor that affects the population fluctuations of many aphids in the Middle East is their susceptibility to khamsins. Due to this and other local climatic conditions, numerous species that infest perennials remain on their hosts throughout the year and reproduce only by parthenogenesis.

Economic Importance: The aphids, which are phloem feeders, include many major plant pests. Their damage is due to sucking out nutrients from host plants and injecting salivary toxins into them, inducing the formation of galls, secreting copious amounts of honey dew that serve as substrates for sooty mold fungi, and to transmitting harmful plant viruses. They attack annuals as well as perennials and cause much damage to greenhouse and house plants, nut and fruit trees and to forests.

Distribution and Ecology: Aphids occur in nearly all available botanical habitats from the tundra to the tropics and infest all parts of the host including the leaves, branches, trunks, fruits, and roots. Their natural enemies include many generalist predators belonging to the Coleoptera, Hemiptera and Neuroptera, as well as hymenopterous endoparasitoids, especially of the family Aphidiidae, which are specialized on aphids.
