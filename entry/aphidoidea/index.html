Aphidoidea

Aphidoidea

Taxonomic placing: Insecta, Hemimetabola, Hemiptera, Sternorrhyncha.

Common name: Aphids, plant lice.

Geographical distribution: World-wide, with more species in the Northern temperate regions. This superfamily consists of close to 5,000 described species that are usually (although not universally) assigned to three families: Adelgidae (=Chermesidae), Phylloxeridae and Aphididae. The former two are sometimes separated from the latter family because they reproduce only by depositing eggs, whereas the Aphididae lay eggs as well as being viviparous.

Morphology: Relatively small, soft-skinned insects with a clear separation into head, thorax, or abdomen, and functional sucking mouth parts in both sexes. The long rostrum consists of 4-5 segments and the antennae have 3-6 segments. The translucent wings, when present, bear reduced venation. The fifth or sixth abdominal segments carry a pair of dorsal funnel-like appendages, the siphunculi (or cornicles) and the hind segment has a tail-like appendage, termed cauda. Adults may be alate (winged) or apterous (lacking wings), and a few species are polymorphic in their larval stages.

Life Histories: All Aphidoidea are plant feeders and usually undergo four nymphal stages. They show a diversity of developmental histories, species alternating between parthenogenetic and sexual generations; the occurrence of the latter is often induced by changes in daylight length and temperature. Many species shift when winged between unrelated host plants. This may be induced by crowding, degradation of food resources, temperature and photoperiod. Many species are attended and protected by ants, which exploit the aphid’s honeydew and concurrently hinder the biological control of pest species.

Economic Importance: The aphids, which are phloem feeders, include many major plant pests. Damage may from depletion of nutrients from host plants, injection salivary toxins that induce deformation, including galls, secreting copious amounts of honey dew that serve as substrates to sooty mold fungi, and the transmission of many plant viruses. Aphids attack annuals as well as perennials and cause much damage to nut and fruit trees, forests, and greenhouse crops, including ornamentals.

Distribution and Ecology: Aphids occur in nearly all available botanical habitats, from the tundra to the tropics, and are found on all parts of host plants: leaves, branches, trunks, fruits, and roots.
