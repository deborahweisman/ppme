Aphytis holoxanthus

Aphytis holoxanthus DeBach

Taxonomic placing: , Holometabola, Hymenoptera, Chalcidoidea, Aphelinidae.

Geographical distribution: Of South-Eastern Asia origin, this species was successfully introduced into Israel in 1956/57 and later into Australia, Florida, South Africa and South America.

Host: The Florida red scale, Chrysomphalus aonidum (L.) (Diaspididae).

Economic importance: An efficient natural enemy of the Florida red scale.

Morphology: The female is yellow, its size being from 0.7 to 0.9 mm.

Life cycle: Like other members of its genus, Aphytis holoxanthus is an ectoparasitoid of armored scale insects. The eggs are deposited through the shield on the host’s body; due to the fact that when molting the shields of these scales adhere to their bodies, the parasitoid can deposit only between molts. Its emerging larvae feed on the host and kill it; prior to pupation the larvae excrete their meconia. The pupa is free, and its eyes change from colorless to red to green, providing an indication of age. The adult emerges through an exit hole in the shield or by crawling out from underneath. It attacks 2nd-stage female scales, male pupae but mostly adult females, on which two or three juveniles may develop. It is an arrhenotokous species, requiring a fortnight to complete a generation at 28Â°C, each female producing about 35 offspring.

Aphytis holoxanthus has not become established in the inner, arid valleys of Israel. When co-occurring with another natural enemy of the Florida red scale, the endoparasitoid Pteroptrix smithi (Compere), the latter appears to be dominant. This may reflect the latter’s ability to parasitize all scale stages (whereas A. holoxanthus can attack the host only between its molts), to the reduced activity of A. holoxanthus on citrus fruit, and to its greater susceptibility to organophosphate pesticides. On the other hand, the parasite has not been affected by the acaricide dicofol or by juvenile hormone analoge IGR.
